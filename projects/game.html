Retro Game Collection

Built in GameMaker Studio 2.

Overview

Built in GameMaker Studio 2, this project includes retro classics like Asteroids, Pong, Atari Breakout, and Snake. All the games were custom designed and coded in GML Code.

Purpose

With the rise of alternative game engines like Godot and GameMaker, I wanted to try my hands on one of them, having only experience on Unity. I chose GameMaker Studio 2 as it was a popular choice for 2D games, and has many available resources.

Process

I first started with deciding what game to make. Pong and Snake were games I had in my generation growing up, so I wanted to pay tribute to my nostalgic beginnings. I then decided on Asteroids and Atari Breakout, as though they were just outside my generation, they were still classics that many people felt nostalgic about.

From there, I started with Asteroids by following a simple tutorial from the GameMaker website. It was a great way to understand basic concepts. At first, I didn't understand the reason behind certain choices GameMaker developers made, such as using key events instead of reading key inputs from the step event. But as I progressed, I understood the importance of these choices.

After Asteroids, I moved on to Pong, which was a bit more challenging. I had to understand how to make the ball bounce off the paddles and walls, and how to make the paddles move. Moving the paddles were easy enough, and I discovered the use of the clamp function, which prevents the paddles from moving out of bounds. The ball bouncing was quitea challenge, as I had to understand basic math concepts to calclate the ball bounce trajectories. The last part of Pong was the AI opponent, which was easy enough. I simply made the AI follow the ball's X position, and locked it to a set movement speed slightly slower than the player's.

Atari Breakout was next. This was by far the easiest to code aside from the Asteroids tutorial I followed. I already had the paddle movement and the ball bounce mechanics, so all I had to do was code the tiles to break once the ball hit them.

Finally, I moved on to Snake. This was the most challenging of the four games, as I had to understand how to make the snake grow when it eats the apple, and how to make the snake move in the direction the player inputs. I also had to make sure the snake didn't collide with itself, which was a challenge in itself. I had to understand how to make the snake move in a grid, and how to make the snake grow in the direction it was moving. All in all, it was a great learning journey with many, many, many trial and errors.
